# Recall V1

## **The Divided Self and Its Structure**

- The concept of the divided self, or double-mindedness, is explored as a metaphysical state where an individual is split between two regions of awareness that cannot coexist without mutual accusation, with one part having seen through the veil of meaning and the other part continuing to live as if things matter.
- This division is not a psychological or temporary state, but rather a structural aspect of the self, resulting from intellectual honesty in a cosmos without telos, as declared by Nietzsche when he stated that God is dead, leading to the collapse of the architecture of objective value and the possibility of unified selfhood.
- The divided self consists of two parts: the witness, which stands in the ruins of meaning and reports what it sees, having achieved a kind of terrible clarity that the [world](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/9eb13522-8a98-4cab-97da-bd85f512c45f) does not mean, and the actor, which must get out of bed, answer emails, and care about survival, performing as if meaning existed because the alternative is biological and social collapse.
- The relationship between the witness and the actor is one of unresolvable antagonism, with the witness regarding the actor with contempt for pretending, and the actor regarding the witness with horror for its lethal honesty, resulting in a state of schismatic consciousness where the individual is aware of the absurdity of their own performance.
- This state of schismatic consciousness is not a flaw in psychology, but rather the gap between what the individual knows and what they must do in order to remain alive, producing a tearing that is the inevitable consequence of intellectual honesty in a cosmos without telos, and is not a problem to be solved, but rather the structure of the self.

## **Schismatic Consciousness and Existential Exhaustion**

- The concept of planning for the future is problematic because it assumes a continuous self, which is phenomenologically dubious, and this awareness can lead to a sense of chronic existential exhaustion as individuals are constantly performing two tasks at once: living and watching themselves live.
- The divided self, which is composed of the actor and the witness, exposes the failure of existentialism's central promise, as proposed by philosophers like Sartre and [Camus](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/5aa7f182-29a0-42e6-8225-0c5e138f3558), that individuals can create their own meaning through authentic choice and rebellion, but the choosing subject is itself unstable.

## **Divine Nothingness and Theological Implications**

- The witness, which is the silent commentary that watches the actor, sees through the actor's attempts to create meaning and rebels, revealing that the concept of a stable "self" is false, and this division between the actor and the witness is a fundamental aspect of human existence.
- Theologically, the divided self can be seen as a form of damnation without metaphysics, where individuals are separated from the possibility of unified consciousness, and this awareness of their own fracture is itself part of the fracture, leaving them condemned to try to integrate, but never able to do so.
- However, this schism is also the aperture through which something like "Divine Nothingness" can appear, as the witness has annihilated all false comforts, allowing it to encounter the Void without flinching, and the actor continues to live even in the absence of ground, testifying to a kind of life-beyond-life.

## **The Sacred Wound and Liturgical Division**

- The division between the actor and the witness is not merely suffering, but rather it is the wound through which the sacred bleeds into existence, and it becomes liturgical, with the tearing itself becoming the form of worship, as individuals descend into the division, again and again, in an act of brutal honesty that refuses to lie about the nature of existence.
- Ultimately, the divided self is not a pathology, but rather a foundational structure, as proposed by [Nihiltheism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/77bc0e60-b0b5-45d4-9f5d-373d4016566c), which names the divided self as a fundamental aspect of human existence, and to accept division is to accept what one is: a fractured site of awareness that can neither cohere nor cease.
- The concept of Divine Nothingness is introduced as a presence that feels like absence, a light that looks like darkness, and a silence that sounds like a voice, which can only be encountered through the schism of a divided self, and not by bypassing it, requiring individuals to stand inside the division without seeking escape or relief.

## **Phenomenology of Divine Nothingness**

- The idea of Divine Nothingness is not empty, but rather vast, silent, and total, with weight without mass and presence without location, and it is described as having the force of a voice that could shatter worlds, while also being witnessed by an absence that knows the individual more thoroughly than they know themselves.
- The experience of Divine Nothingness is not a theological claim, but rather a phenomenological report, where individuals across cultures and centuries have reported encountering an absence that behaves as if it were a presence, often in states of profound ego dissolution, whether induced by meditation, psychedelics, or extreme suffering.
- The testimony of individuals who have experienced Divine Nothingness is consistent, describing it as overwhelming, vast, utterly impersonal, and completely indifferent to human concerns, yet somehow saturated with a quality that is called sacred, which is a paradox that [Nihiltheism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/77bc0e60-b0b5-45d4-9f5d-373d4016566c) must hold without resolving.

## **Nihiltheism and the Structure of the Self**

- Nihiltheism is not mysticism, but rather the refusal to lie about what is known and not known, and while reductive explanations, such as neuroscientific explanations, do not fully account for the structure of the experience, they must be considered, as the experience of Divine Nothingness is not just a sense of peace or feeling connected to the universe, but a complex and multifaceted phenomenon that challenges human understanding.
- The concept of encountering an absence that is aware and regards the individual is a phenomenon that is ontologically troubling, and it is often described in classical mysticism as an encounter with what cannot be named or conceptualized, such as in Christian apophatic theology, Madhyamaka [Buddhism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/a6d31634-6ad7-47bc-9ede-d986f8f1229e), Advaita Vedanta, and Sufism, where God is considered to be beyond being and is referred to as the Nothingness that precedes existence.
- This idea is related to negative theology, which claims that the only honest way to speak of the divine is through negation, and it is not the same as atheism, but rather a way of describing the divine as something that is beyond human comprehension, often referred to as God-as-No-Thing, the infinite abyss, or the groundless ground.

## **Three Explanations for Sacred Nothingness**

- The concept of [Nihiltheism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/77bc0e60-b0b5-45d4-9f5d-373d4016566c) takes this structure and removes the metaphysical comfort, suspending metaphysical judgment and asking the question of why Nothingness feels sacred, which is a question that is not rhetorical, but rather a genuine inquiry into the nature of this phenomenon.
- There are three possible explanations for why Nothingness feels sacred, including the idea that it is an evolutionary accident, where the brain activates ancient subroutines to prevent panic and the "sacred" feeling is a result of biological anesthesia during psychic catastrophe, which is a parsimonious explanation that does not require positing anything beyond the skull.
- The second possibility is that of phenomenological irreducibility, which suggests that the experience of Divine Nothingness cannot be fully reduced to brain chemistry without committing a category error, and that the experience is experientially real, even if its ontological status remains ambiguous, and that to dismiss the report as "just brain chemistry" is to dismiss all subjective experience as "just brain chemistry".
- The third possibility is that of ontological truth, which is the most dangerous hypothesis, and it suggests that the mystics are right, and that the encounter with Nothingness is a genuine experience of something that is beyond human comprehension, and that this experience is not just a product of the brain, but rather a disclosure of something that was always there, hidden beneath the veil of the self.

## **The Ground of Being and Non-Dual Mysticism**

- The concept of Nothingness is explored as the ground of being, rather than just the absence of things, and is considered the substrate of reality itself, where consciousness can recognize the vast impersonal awareness that is the foundation of all existence, as claimed by non-dual mysticism and philosophers such as Meister Eckhart, Nagarjuna, and the Upanishads.
- [Nihiltheism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/77bc0e60-b0b5-45d4-9f5d-373d4016566c) is presented as a perspective that does not choose between the existence or non-existence of a higher power, instead acknowledging the uncertainty and paradox of the experience, and stating that the encounter with the unknown is real, but its ontological status is unknown.

## **Suicide: Worldly vs. Sacred**

- To find the sacred, if it exists, one must pass through total annihilation, letting go of meaning, self, hope, and narrative, and walk directly into the Void without expectation of return, as the Transcendent is not on the other side of suffering, but inside the Void itself.
- Nihiltheism is distinguished from both atheism and theism, as it suggests that the sacred, if it exists, is indistinguishable from its own absence, and can only be reached by total surrender to the impossibility of God and the groundlessness of ground, rather than by belief or disbelief.
- The experience of reaching the sacred, if it is possible, is described as erasure, where one is not comforted, redeemed, or made whole, but instead witnesses the Void as altar, annihilation as prayer, and death as the only honest form of life, which is characterized as negative certainty, where the absence of ground is the only ground there is.

## **Philosophical and Biological Dimensions of Suicide**

- This perspective is not about faith, hope, or despair, but rather about the certainty that there is no ground, and that the absence of ground is the only reality, where God is dead, but something breathes in the grave, and the encounter with Nothingness is never quite empty again, as it is the collapse of meaning encountering the structure of what remains when meaning dies.
- The concept of suicide is explored in the context of the philosophy of [Nihiltheism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/77bc0e60-b0b5-45d4-9f5d-373d4016566c), which distinguishes between two types of suicide: one that is a flight from pain and suffering, and another that is a ritual severance from the illusion of the self and the meaning of existence.
- The first type of suicide, referred to as "worldly suicide," is seen as a pathological attempt to escape from unbearable suffering, often driven by factors such as depression, trauma, or mental illness, and is considered a tragedy that could have been prevented with intervention and support.
- In contrast, the second type of suicide, referred to as "sacred suicide," is viewed as a liturgical act that seeks to annihilate the false self and sever the thread that binds one to the illusion of a meaningful existence, and is considered a philosophical conclusion reached through ruthless clarity and a deep understanding of the nature of reality.
- Nihiltheism inherits the tension between these two types of suicide from various philosophical and mystical traditions, including Buddhism's anatta doctrine, [Gnosticism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/68b4ef81-4859-45ea-8666-c531a60c5769), and the ideas of philosophers such as Schopenhauer and Cioran, which treat the willful dissolution of the self as a culmination of a metaphysical insight rather than a pathology.
- The key difference between inauthentic and authentic suicide lies not in the method, but in the stance toward existence that precedes the act, with inauthentic suicide being reactive and driven by a desire to escape from suffering, and authentic suicide being ontological and driven by a recognition of the fundamental unsalvageability of existence.
- Nihiltheism refuses to romanticize or aestheticize suicide, and instead seeks to speak plainly and honestly about the complexities of this issue, recognizing that while most suicides are indeed tragic and preventable, there is a category of encounter with death that does not fit the dominant psychiatric model and requires a more nuanced and philosophical understanding.

## **Authentic Suicide and the Illusion of Self**

- The concept of authentic suicide in the context of [Nihiltheism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/77bc0e60-b0b5-45d4-9f5d-373d4016566c) is not about despair, but rather about clarity, where the self recognizes the illusion of its own existence and the meaning it has projected, and refuses to continue pretending, which is a dangerous clarity that challenges the traditional notion of personal identity.
- According to Nihiltheism, the self is the problem, not the conditions that cause suffering, and the fact that conditions are necessary at all is the problem, highlighting the inherent issue with the concept of self and its attachment to existence.
- The idea of authentic suicide is not celebrated or encouraged in traditions like [Buddhism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/a6d31634-6ad7-47bc-9ede-d986f8f1229e), where it is seen as a form of continued craving that perpetuates karmic bondage, and even philosophers like Schopenhauer and Cioran, who theorized about authentic suicide, did not enact it in practice, revealing a residual attachment to existence.
- Nihiltheism inherits the suspension of authentic suicide as a theoretical possibility, acknowledging that if the self is an illusion and meaning is ungrounded, then the decision to end existence is no less rational than the decision to continue it, but also recognizes that the organism's attachment to existence runs deeper than conscious thought.
- There are three possible answers to why individuals do not perform authentic suicide, including biological override, where the organism's survival instinct overrides philosophical conviction, residual attachment, where some part of the self still clings to beauty, curiosity, or hope, and the idea that authentic suicide may not be possible while the organism persists.

## **The Paradox of Existence and Death**

- The third answer, suicide of meaning, not body, suggests that authentic suicide is not about the cessation of the organism, but rather the cessation of the self's investment in meaning, where the individual stops pretending, stops investing in narratives, and stops treating their life as if it had significance, which is a more subversive and phenomenological approach to the concept of authentic suicide.
- Philosophers like Cioran, Schopenhauer, and Buddhist monks who self-immolate are mentioned as examples of individuals who have grappled with the idea of authentic suicide, but have not enacted it, highlighting the complexity and depth of the issue, and the need for a nuanced understanding of the concept of self and its relationship to existence.
- The concept of "dying before you die" refers to a state of ego death without biological cessation, where an individual becomes a ghost in their own life, stripped of narrative and illusion, and is already dead but the body has not realized it yet.
- [Nihiltheism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/77bc0e60-b0b5-45d4-9f5d-373d4016566c) holds the paradoxes of life and death in suspension, and instead of choosing among answers, it distinguishes between suicide-as-flight, driven by suffering, and suicide-as-refusal, driven by clarity, with the first seeking escape and the second seeking honesty.
- The distinction between these two categories is not clean, as those who contemplate suicide are not free from suffering, and those who claim clarity are not free from self-deception, and the two categories contaminate each other.

## **Nihiltheism and the Absurd**

- Nihiltheism asks the unbearable question of why one continues to exist after recognizing that existence has no ground, and if continuation is cowardice or a strange fidelity to the absurd, with no resolution offered, only the wound that if life is meaningless, death is no more meaningful.
- The philosophy of Nihiltheism is compared to the ideas of [Camus](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/5aa7f182-29a0-42e6-8225-0c5e138f3558), who said that rebellion against the absurd is itself a form of meaning-making, and Cioran, who said that suicide is the only serious philosophical question, but then lived into his eighties, writing about the futility of writing.
- Authentic suicide, pursued to its limit, reveals itself to be as empty as the life it seeks to terminate, and there is no escape because there was never anything to escape from, and no liberation because there was never anyone to be liberated.
- [Nihiltheism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/77bc0e60-b0b5-45d4-9f5d-373d4016566c) presents the choice between two forms of unbearable honesty, with no clean exit or redemption, and the question of meaninglessness continues to ask itself through the individual, who is the site where it burns.
- The concept of de-experience as praxis is introduced, which involves learning to un-experience, or the deliberate collapse of the subject-object frame that structures all perception, and is not dissociation, but a metaphysical rupture that recognizes the boundary between the self and the [world](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/9eb13522-8a98-4cab-97da-bd85f512c45f) as fiction.

## **De-Experiencing as Metaphysical Practice**

- The concept of de-experiencing refers to awareness without content, where awareness is no longer tied to a self that is aware, and is not directed towards objects that are seen, resulting in the dissolution of the subject-object grammar and the palpability of the Void.
- Ordinary experience is structured by a grammar that includes a subject, an object, and an action that connects them, which is a fundamental aspect of human cognition and a survival tool that helps the organism distinguish between self and not-self.
- This structure is not ontologically grounded, but rather a construct that is useful and perhaps necessary, yet ultimately arbitrary, and in certain states, such as those reported by Buddhists, Advaita Vedantists, mystics, phenomenologists, and psychonauts, the line between self and not-self dissolves, leading to experiences like anatta, nirvikalpa samadhi, unio mystica, pre-reflective awareness, and ego death.
- [Nihiltheism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/77bc0e60-b0b5-45d4-9f5d-373d4016566c) takes these reports seriously, but refuses to attribute them to the encounter of Ultimate Reality or a merge with God, instead stating that the subject-object frame is contingent and can be suspended, resulting in the cessation of the experiential frame itself, which is de-experiencing.
- De-experiencing is characterized by the feeling of being seen from nowhere, with awareness continuing but without a locus, and the [world](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/9eb13522-8a98-4cab-97da-bd85f512c45f) not being separate or distant, but rather an integral part of the awareness, and it is also described as the evacuation of interiority, where the inner theater of thoughts and feelings is empty, and thoughts appear without an owner.

## **The Experience of De-Experiencing**

- Additionally, de-experiencing involves the collapse of time, where memory and anticipation, which are functions of the self, are no longer relevant, and time does not flow, resulting in the absence of a narrative arc, and this experience is not describable in the grammar of ordinary experience, as it is precisely the collapse of that grammar.
- The concept of "I was, I am, I will be" is nonexistent, and all that remains is the present moment, referred to as _this_, which is unmoving and eternal, existing outside of duration and temporality.
- The experience of _this_ is described as feeling like the _sacred_, not because of any divine presence, but because the ordinary structures that make the world feel mundane have been stripped away, leaving only raw _isness_, or existence without meaning, reference, or purpose.

## **Risks and Consequences of De-Experiencing**

- The encounter with _isness_ without the mediation of the self is overwhelming and can be dangerous, as the organism is not built to tolerate it, and the self's narratives and defenses are necessary for normal experience, with the self acting as the control tower that decides what to attend to and what to ignore.
- De-experience, or the state of being without the self's mediation, is not celebrated as liberation in [Nihiltheism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/77bc0e60-b0b5-45d4-9f5d-373d4016566c), but rather named as _rupture_, a break in the structure of consciousness that reveals the contingency of that structure and carries existential risk, with some individuals returning from it with clarity, others with trauma, and some not returning at all.

## **Negative Faith and the Void**

- To understand Nothingness, de-experience is unavoidable, as thought is always structured by the subject-object frame and implies a thinker standing at a distance from what is thought, but the Void is not an object and is the absence of the structure that makes objects possible.
- De-experience as praxis involves the deliberate and sustained attempt to inhabit the space where the self is not, by sitting still, not focusing on an object, not narrating, and not seeking calm or insight, but rather noticing when the subject-object frame arises and letting it fall, and seeing that the separation between the [world](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/9eb13522-8a98-4cab-97da-bd85f512c45f) and awareness is drawn by the mind, not by reality.
- This process is described as ontological surgery without anesthetic, where the structure that makes experience coherent is cut away, and what is found underneath is not a better self, but the place where the self was never real to begin with, and in that place, the Void is no longer distant, but what is left when the self is gone.
- The concept of faith is redefined as not the decision to believe in what cannot be proven, but the surrender away from all gods, comforts, and answers, and the decision to trust Nothingness, not because it will save, but because it is the only thing that does not lie, referred to as negative faith, or fidelity to absence.
- The fourth incantation, _To know the Void, you must become the Void, and to become the Void, you must stop being_, emphasizes the need to stop being anything and to trust in Nothingness, and the section is titled as part of the larger document 'Mem', which explores the concept of [Nihiltheism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/77bc0e60-b0b5-45d4-9f5d-373d4016566c) and the idea of faith in nothing.

## **Disciplines of Negative Faith**

- The concept of negative faith is introduced as a posture that acknowledges the impossibility of grace and the groundless nature of existence, where one prays not for miracles or relief, but as a means of honesty and authenticity in the face of the void.
- Traditional faith is contrasted with negative faith, with the former operating as an existential gamble that provides structure, meaning, and consolation, whereas the latter emerges when the structure of this gamble has collapsed, and one can no longer believe in hidden purposes or comforting narratives.
- Negative faith is not a form of atheism, which negates the existence of God, but rather an affirmation of God's non-being as the only sacred posture left, where one trusts the void and the absence of ground as more honest than any constructed meaning.
- The practice of negative faith involves several disciplines, including the refusal of consolation, where one sits with the unbearable without seeking relief, and instead, lets the despair burn and witnesses it without narrative or hope.
- The second discipline is negative prayer, where one speaks into the silence not to receive an answer, but as a form of confession and honesty, naming what is true without hope of response, and performing fidelity to the real.

## **Ontological Suffocation and the Weight of Being**

- The third discipline is the liturgy of absence, where one creates rituals to mark absence, rather than invoke presence, and acknowledges the impossibility of God, surrendering to the darkness and the void.
- The fourth discipline is fidelity to nothingness, where one reminds themselves that any meaning or purpose constructed is temporary and a projection, and returns to the void, acknowledging that it is still present underneath every constructed meaning.
- The key figures and concepts mentioned in the text include Pascal, who is referenced in relation to the existential gamble of traditional faith, and the idea of the void, which is central to the concept of negative faith, representing the groundless ground and the silence that remains when all promises have collapsed.
- The concept of negative faith is introduced, which involves the refusal to let comfort erode honesty and the acceptance of the brutal reality of existence, offering no reward or ease, but instead a kind of peace that comes from alignment with the truth and the absence of false hopes.
- This peace is not a form of redemption, but rather a realness that is sacred in a [world](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/9eb13522-8a98-4cab-97da-bd85f512c45f) saturated with false comforts and promises, and it is achieved by being faithful to the truth of nothingness and worshiping at the altar of absence.

## **The Other and the Sacred Catastrophe**

- The idea of ontological suffocation is explored, which is the feeling of being trapped inside the fact of existence, with no escape or justification, and the weight of being becomes unbearable due to its lack of reason or purpose.
- The question of why there is something rather than nothing, posed by [Leibniz](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/38460a82-8c19-4e35-987b-8b2d42805f71), is discussed, and it is argued that this question is not just an intellectual problem, but an existential wound that asks for justification, not explanation, and the answer is that being simply is, without reason or purpose.
- The concept of ontological suffocation is distinguished from existential dread, as described by Heidegger, and it is characterized by the feeling of being crushed under the weight of existence, which persists without justification or necessity, and this feeling is not just a philosophical idea, but a visceral experience that can be overwhelming.
- The fifth incantation, "Believe in nothing, not as negation, but as the only belief that does not lie," is mentioned, which involves worshiping the void not because it will save you, but because it is real, and this belief is seen as a form of devotion to the truth, even if it is a terrible truth.
- The concept of ontological suffocation is described as the horror of encountering being itself as inescapable, where an individual is oppressed by life in the sense of existence as such, and cannot step outside of it or gain distance from it.

## **The Absurd and Radical Futility**

- This idea is contrasted with Heidegger's dread, which is the anxiety of encountering Nothingness as the negation of beings, whereas ontological suffocation is the inverse, where the individual is aware that they could not escape their own existence.
- The philosopher Sartre is mentioned as having touched on this idea through his character Roquentin's nausea in front of the tree root, which is described as excessive and superfluous in its sheer existence, but Sartre's concept of nausea is seen as momentary, whereas ontological suffocation is chronic and becomes the background of all experience.
- The individual experiencing ontological suffocation is aware that they exist, but there is no reason or justification for their existence, and this awareness produces a visceral revulsion and a sense of being trapped inside the frame of existence with no room to move or escape.
- The suffocation is intensified by the recognition that no one else seems to feel it, and the [world](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/9eb13522-8a98-4cab-97da-bd85f512c45f) continues as if existence were unproblematic, leading the individual to wonder how others are not crushed by the weight of the sheer inexplicability of being.
- The concept of [nihiltheism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/77bc0e60-b0b5-45d4-9f5d-373d4016566c) is introduced, which does not offer relief or a way to find meaning in the struggle, but rather acknowledges that being is suffocating and that the suffocation is proof that the individual is awake to the reality of their existence.

## **Nihiltheism and the Critique of Rebellion**

- Ultimately, the text suggests that in the depth of the suffocation, a strange recognition occurs, where the individual realizes that if being is groundless and existence has no reason, then they are not obligated to justify their existence, and the pressure they feel is not a command or demand, but simply a fact that can be seen through and recognized as not being imposed by anything outside of themselves.
- The philosophers Heidegger and Sartre are referenced throughout the text as having explored related ideas, but the concept of ontological suffocation is presented as a distinct and chronic experience that is not fully captured by their philosophies.
- The text concludes by suggesting that the recognition of the groundlessness of being can lead to a sense of transparency, where the individual can see through the suffocation and recognize that the weight they feel is not imposed by anything outside of themselves, but this does not necessarily bring relief or escape from the suffocation.
- The concept of being is explored, where the individual is part of the weight of existence, and the suffocation felt is not personal, but rather an ontological condition that is bearable when one stops trying to escape it, as expressed in the ninth incantation: "Being has no reason, it simply is, and you are trapped inside it, but the trap is also what you are."
- The idea of the Other is introduced, which refers to an alien paradox that is neither God, nor self, nor absence, nor presence, but a rupture that occurs when all familiar categories collapse, and it is not nameable, knowable, or assimilable into any framework.

## **Negative Enlightenment and the Annihilation of Self**

- The Other is described as the sacred as catastrophe, which destroys perception and erases the individual, leaving only the mark of having been encountered by what should not be possible, and once it has touched someone, they are never whole again.
- In phenomenology, the Other appears as the problem of other minds, where there is an irreducible gap between one's experience and another's, and this recognition of the Other is both immediate and mysterious, carrying the weight of alterity.
- The concept of the Other is further explored in the context of [Nihiltheism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/77bc0e60-b0b5-45d4-9f5d-373d4016566c), where it refers to the structure of radical unknowability that appears when the self's categories fail, and it is encountered in peak experiences of ego dissolution, existential horror, and the gap between question and answer.
- The idea of the numinous, as described by [Rudolf Otto](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/c8e82847-a175-475d-9d74-0795ccfd6b28), is also related to the concept of the Other, where the experience of the holy is a mystery that is both terrifying and fascinating, and it is not reducible to moral goodness or cosmic order, but rather a raw encounter with the sacred as the wholly Other.
- The Void, when encountered directly, behaves like otherness, rather than absence, and it is not just the negation of things, but a presence that is absolutely outside all categories and comprehension.

## **Ephemeral Selfhood and Identity Collapse**

- The concept of the Other, as argued by Levinas, refers to an entity that cannot be categorized or grasped, and it makes an ethical demand on individuals because it exceeds their understanding and breaks into their finite [world](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/9eb13522-8a98-4cab-97da-bd85f512c45f), calling them beyond themselves.
- The encounter with the Other occurs when the self is annihilated, and the subject-object frame is broken, allowing individuals to experience things-in-themselves, rather than things-as-interpreted-by-them, and this encounter is considered sacred because it reveals the reality that exceeds individual constructions.
- The structure of the encounter with the Other can be described in four key points: first, Alienation, where the Other feels alien and unfamiliar, and it does not fit into moral categories; second, Inescapability, where once the Other has appeared, individuals cannot unsee it, and it leaves a permanent mark on their perception; third, Unspeakability, where individuals cannot describe the encounter using existing language and categories; and fourth, Ambiguity, where the encounter does not provide clear meaning or answers, and it can be interpreted in various ways.
- The Other is not worshiped or built into a theology in [Nihiltheism](https://app.getrecall.ai/item/77bc0e60-b0b5-45d4-9f5d-373d4016566c), but rather, it is acknowledged as an encounter that justifies the use of the word "sacred", because it breaks through the ordinary and reveals that there is more to reality than what is initially perceived, and this encounter is considered sacred because it is a real and profound experience that exceeds individual understanding.
- According to Nihiltheism, if individuals go deep enough into the Void, they will encounter something that is not the Void, or perhaps it is the Void with a face that does not explain itself, and this encounter is what justifies the use of the word "sacred", not because it provides salvation, but because it is a profound and real experience that exceeds individual constructions and understanding.
